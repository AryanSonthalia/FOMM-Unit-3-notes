Section 01

Introduction to the STP Model

Traditional (Product-Oriented):
"Sell what we produce."
Modern (Customer-Oriented):
"Produce what customers want."

Instead of treating the entire market as one large group, companies identify specific customer groups, select the most attractive ones, and develop offerings that match their requirements.

Meaning of Each Component

🔷 Segmentation

Dividing a large heterogeneous market into smaller homogeneous groups of customers.

🎯 Targeting

Selecting the most attractive segment(s) for business focus and resource allocation.

📌 Positioning

Creating a distinctive image in the minds of customers relative to competitors.

Levels of Market Segmentation

A company cannot effectively serve every customer in a large market. Depending on resources, product type, market conditions, and competitive intensity, companies adopt different levels of segmentation.

Hierarchy — Broadest to Narrowest Focus

A. Mass Marketing (Undifferentiated Marketing)

Definition

Mass marketing involves mass production, mass distribution, and mass promotion of a single product for all consumers. Every customer is treated similarly and individual differences are largely ignored.

Characteristics

  • Standardized product for all
  • Single marketing program
  • Large-scale production
  • Broad customer base

✅ Advantages

AdvantageExplanation
Economies of ScaleLower cost per unit
SimplicityEasier management
Wider ReachLarger audience
Lower Marketing CostsOne campaign for all

❌ Limitations

LimitationExplanation
Ignores DifferencesDiverse needs not addressed
Lower PersonalizationGeneric offerings
Strong CompetitionDifficult differentiation

Examples

Example — Henry Ford

Model T Strategy:

"You can have any color you want, as long as it is black."
Example — India

Amul Butter and Tata Salt historically operated using mass marketing principles — one product for the entire Indian market.

B. Segment Marketing (Differentiated Marketing)

Definition

The company divides the market into broad segments and develops different offerings for one or more segments. Customers have different needs, so groups with similar needs are clustered together and served distinctly.

✅ Advantages

  • Higher customer satisfaction
  • Better product fit
  • Stronger brand loyalty
  • Improved market coverage

⚠️ Challenges

  • Higher costs due to multiple programs
  • More complex management
  • Multiple marketing programs required

C. Niche Marketing (Concentrated Marketing)

Definition

A company focuses on a narrowly defined group of customers with specialized needs. Niches are smaller than broad segments. The philosophy: "Be a big fish in a small pond — focus deeply rather than broadly."

Characteristics

  • Specialized offerings tailored to a specific need
  • Limited competition from generalist brands
  • Highly focused, targeted marketing efforts
AdvantageExplanation
Premium PricingCustomers are willing to pay more for specialized solutions
Strong LoyaltyBetter satisfaction leads to repeat purchase
Lower CompetitionFew rivals operate in the same niche
Deep ExpertiseCompany builds specialization and credibility
Example 1 — Sensodyne

Focuses specifically on consumers with sensitive teeth, rather than competing in general oral care. Commands premium pricing and strong loyalty.

Example 2 — Ananda Healthcare

Focuses exclusively on Luxury Ayurveda Wellness Retreats — a highly defined niche within the broader wellness/hospitality market.

D. Micro Marketing (Local & Individual Marketing)

Definition

Micro marketing involves tailoring products and marketing programs to local groups, specific communities, or individual customers.

📍 Local Marketing

Products are customized according to the needs of local communities or geographic clusters.

Example — McDonald's India

Offers McAloo Tikki and Maharaja Mac — products that differ significantly from U.S. offerings to suit Indian tastes and preferences.

🧍 Individual Marketing

Also known as One-to-One Marketing, Segment of One, or Mass Customization. Products are designed for each individual customer.

Examples

Nike By You: Customers design their own shoes.
Corporate Gifting: Products customized per client specifications.

Section 03

Bases of Consumer Market Segmentation

Marketers use specific variables to divide a large market into meaningful groups. The four major segmentation bases are:

🗺️ Geographic

Nation, state, region, city, climate, density

👥 Demographic

Age, gender, income, occupation, education

🧠 Psychographic

Lifestyle, personality, values, social class

⚡ Behavioral

Occasions, benefits, loyalty, usage rate

A. Geographic Segmentation

Definition

Dividing markets based on geographic boundaries such as nation, state, region, city, climate, and population density. Consumer needs differ significantly across urban vs rural markets.

Segmentation Variables

Nation State Region City Climate Population Density
Example — Godrej & Voltas

🌡️ Hot regions: Promote Air Conditioners heavily to meet high cooling demand.

❄️ Cold regions: Promote Room Heaters and Water Geysers for warming needs.

B. Demographic Segmentation

Definition

Dividing consumers according to measurable characteristics such as age, gender, income, occupation, education, religion, and family life cycle. This is the most popular segmentation base because these variables are easy to measure.

Segmentation Variables

Age Gender Income Occupation Education Religion Family Life Cycle
Example — Gender Segmentation (L'Oréal)

L'Oréal develops entirely different product lines for Men and Women, recognizing different grooming needs, preferences, and buying motivations.

C. Psychographic Segmentation

Definition

Dividing consumers according to their lifestyle, personality, values, interests, and social class. Key insight: Two consumers may have identical demographics but completely different lifestyles.

Segmentation Variables

Lifestyle Personality Values Interests Social Class
Example — Royal Enfield

Targets consumers who value Adventure, Freedom, and a Rugged Lifestyle. The brand personality is bold, independent, and exploratory.

Example — Honda Activa

Targets consumers who value Practicality, Safety, and Convenience. The brand is associated with reliability and ease of daily use.

Dividing consumers according to their actual behavior toward products. Many marketers consider behavioral segmentation the most useful segmentation base as it directly reflects buying action.

Occasion-Based Segmentation

Benefits Sought

These packs are targeted specifically toward festivals and gifting occasions — a classic example of occasion-based behavioral segmentation. The product design, packaging, and advertising all reflect this behavior context.

Section 04

Targeting

Definition

Targeting is the process of evaluating different market segments and selecting the most attractive segment(s) for business focus and resource allocation. After dividing the market, a company must decide which segment(s) it actually wants to serve.

  • Efficient resource allocation

  • Improved competitive advantage

Evaluating Market Segments

Before selecting a target market, marketers evaluate each segment on five key criteria:

1️⃣ Segment Size

Is the segment large enough to be profitable?

Example: Luxury sports cars may have limited customers compared to commuter motorcycles.

2️⃣ Segment Growth Potential

Will the segment continue growing in the future?

Example: Electric Vehicles represent a rapidly growing segment.

3️⃣ Structural Attractiveness

How intense is the competition? Consider: number of competitors, entry barriers, substitutes, supplier power, and buyer power.

Example: The smartphone market is highly competitive.

4️⃣ Company Objectives

Does the segment align with company goals?

Example: A premium brand may avoid highly price-sensitive segments.

5️⃣ Company Resources

Does the company possess sufficient resources to serve this segment?

Example: Small firms may struggle to compete in mass markets that require heavy ad spend.

Targeting Strategies

A. Undifferentiated Marketing

The company ignores segment differences and targets the entire market with one offer. Philosophy: "One product for all customers."

Economies of Scale Lower Marketing Cost Simpler Management

Example: Traditional Tata Salt strategy.

B. Differentiated Marketing

The company targets multiple segments using different offerings tailored for each group.

Greater Coverage Better Satisfaction Increased Sales

Example: Maruti Suzuki — Alto, WagonR, Swift, Grand Vitara.

C. Concentrated Marketing

The company focuses on one segment or niche. This builds strong expertise and allows premium pricing, but creates vulnerability if the segment shrinks.

Strong Expertise Premium Pricing High Loyalty

Example: Sensodyne — sensitive teeth customers only.

D. Micromarketing

Products and programs are customized for specific local groups or individuals. Highest personalization level.

Local Marketing Individual Marketing Mass Customization

Example: Nike By You — custom shoe design per customer.

Section 05

Positioning

Definition

Positioning is the act of designing a company's offering and image to occupy a distinctive place in the mind of the target customer relative to competitors. It answers: "Why should customers choose our brand instead of competitors?"

Positioning Statement

A positioning statement clearly communicates the brand's intended position in a structured format:

For (target market), our brand is (frame of reference) that provides (benefit) because (reason to believe).
Example — Apple iPhone

For young urban professionals, Apple iPhone is a premium smartphone that delivers superior user experience because of its ecosystem, design, and innovation.

Section 06

Differences Among Segmentation, Targeting & Positioning

STP is a continuous three-step process. Each stage has a distinct objective, question, and outcome:

Feature Segmentation Targeting Positioning
Core Meaning Dividing the market into groups Selecting which groups to serve Creating a brand image
Primary Question How can the market be divided? Which group should be served? Why should customers choose us?
Focus Analytical Strategic Psychological
Main Activity Identify segments Select segments Build perception
Outcome Segment Profiles Target Market Brand Position
Analogy Slicing the cake Choosing the slice Presenting the slice attractively

The Bakery Analogy

🔷 Segmentation

Slicing the whole cake into different pieces based on flavor, size, and ingredients.

🎯 Targeting

Choosing which slice you actually want to eat — deciding which segment is most attractive.

📌 Positioning

Decorating and presenting that slice attractively to make it desirable and distinctive.
